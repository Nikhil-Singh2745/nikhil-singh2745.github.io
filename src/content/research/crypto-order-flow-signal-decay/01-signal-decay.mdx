<DropCap char="A" /> predictive signal is only worth the horizon over which it stays
informative. In execution that horizon is the whole game: a signal that predicts price
ten seconds out is a different instrument from one that predicts it ten milliseconds
out, because the second number has to clear the latency of computing, transmitting, and
placing an order before any edge is left. This note measures the decay of one common
signal, order-flow imbalance, on a liquid crypto pair, and sets up the question that
actually matters: does the surviving edge clear transaction costs.

## context

The signal is a per-second order-flow imbalance proxy. Using one-second bars, define
net aggressive flow as twice the taker-buy base volume minus total base volume, so a
positive value means aggressive buying dominated that second and a negative value means
aggressive selling did. The scale-free version divides by total volume. Both are
standard microstructure constructions.

The data is public: Binance's klines endpoint, one-second interval, symbol BTCUSDT,
6000 bars spanning roughly 100 minutes on 2026-06-26. This is a single pair over a
single window. It is a first look, not a claim about the market in general.

<Aside label="scope">
  One-second bars measure decay at the second scale, not the sub-millisecond scale a
  colocated stack lives in. Probing the fast end needs tick-level order book data, which
  klines cannot provide. That is a deliberate boundary of this measurement, not an
  oversight.
</Aside>

## findings

Predictive power is measured as the information coefficient, the Spearman rank
correlation between the signal at time t and the forward log-return over a horizon of h
seconds. The signed-volume signal gives:

<CodeBlock lang="text" code={`horizon   IC
   1s    +0.073
   2s    +0.120
   3s    +0.135
   5s    +0.137   <- peak
  10s    +0.090
  20s    +0.062
  30s    +0.038
  60s    +0.024
 120s    -0.002
 300s    -0.018`} />

The shape is momentum then reversion. Predictive power climbs to a peak near a
five-second horizon, falls to half its peak by twenty seconds, and turns slightly
negative past two minutes. The late sign flip is consistent with transient price impact
partially reverting after a burst of aggressive flow.

With 6000 observations the standard error on a correlation is about one over the square
root of the sample size, near 0.013, so a peak IC of 0.137 is on the order of ten
standard errors. Within this window it is a real relationship, not sampling noise. The
scale-free imbalance ratio shows the same timing with a lower peak near 0.095, which
suggests the magnitude of flow, not only its direction, carries information.

The signal itself barely persists. Its autocorrelation is near zero within a few
seconds, so the value is not in the signal repeating but in its near-immediate link to
price.

## what this does not show

An information coefficient bounds an opportunity. It is not a profit. A peak at five
seconds says nothing about whether the trade makes money once taker fees, the spread
paid on entry and exit, and market impact are charged against it. That subtraction, the
net-of-cost crossover, is the next step: take the published taker fee, estimate the
half-spread from sampled depth, and ask whether the five to twenty second usable band
survives. A signal can be highly significant and still sit entirely inside the cost
band, in which case it is real and worthless at once.
